Welcome to the forum for the Global Space Balloon Challenge! This forum is the crux of the GSBC community - introduce your team, share your ideas, ask questions, and help others successfully fly their balloons. Anything and everything about HABs is welcome. What is This Forum About?

The Global Space Balloon Challenge (GSBC) is the coming together of people around the world to simultaneously fly high altitude balloons from every corner of the globe, celebrating an age where anyone can reach the edge of space for a few hundred dollars and a few weekends of work. Learn more and join the fun on our website or check out the hundreds of teams from dozens of countries already signed up!

On this forum, get the help you need for your HAB from the “tutorials” and “technical Q&A” categories. Let us know when you are flying throughout the year and what happens through the “launch announcements and results” category so everyone can see your progress! Educators head to the “education” category and if you need anything else or want to share something awesome, check out “general questions and sharing” [link]. For all GSBC information, we’ll be posting to “challenges and previous results” for prize info and results from last year and to “GSBC announcements” for nearly everything else challenge related.

Requirements for Participation in the GSBC

Anyone participating in the Global Space Balloon Challenge must follow these requirements:

  1. You must be part of a team with at least one other member. This is to ensure everyone’s safety as they assemble and fly their balloons.

  2. Your payload must contain a camera. We know this adds weight to your balloon, but we believe sharing photos is the best way to inspire and connect with other teams, a critical component of the GSBC.

  3. Your payload must carry a GPS. This will allow you to track and recover your payload safely.

  4. You must follow all your local regulations. Please see the regulations page for more information.

  5. Challenge submissions must be submitted by no more than 1 week after the challenge window has closed.

  6. Everyone on a team must have signed up on the website and accepted the Terms and Conditions.

    Its quite possible to launch your 2.5kg payload on a single balloon, many people have done similar.

    Most met balloons have published data that specifies their performance for a particular set of conditions: free lift, ascent rate, burst height & payload weight - but the balloons are quite capable of handling other sets of conditions - the manufacturers just don't specify what the results will be. Typically balloons up to 1000g have specifications for a payload weight of 250g and above that for 1050g. This is where your 1050g figure came from.

    The calculator at the bottom of this page:

    http://randomaerospace.com/Random_Aerospace/Balloons.html469

    and here

    http://habhub.org/calc/236

    allows you to try different balloon size, gas type, payload weight & ascent rate combinations - it gives a reasonable estimate of gas volume needed, expected burst height, neck lift etc.

    For example yesterday I launched 1.4Kg in payloads under a small 350g totex balloon. That balloon only has published data for a 250g payload and at a much lower free lift than we were using. The resultant ascent rate and burst altitude were very close to the estimated values (estimated average ascent rate 6m/sec, actual 6.1m/sec, estimated burst 18Km actual 19.2Km).

    Obviously if you have a heavy payload on a small balloon and/or you fill it with a lot of gas (to get a fast ascent) it wont get very high before it bursts. So how far can you push it? That would depend on the balloon construction, make, size and type. Personally as a rule of thumb I wouldn't normally go for more than about 2 times the balloon weight.

    A Totex TA-1500, Hwoyee 1600 or Pawan 2000 would probably be a good choice for that sort of payload weight - but ask around and see what has worked for others.

    Multiple Balloons: I would generally avoid using more than one balloon unless you have a reliable release mechanism - one of the balloons is liable to burst first and the result might be a "float" or a very gradual descent to the ground over several hours.

    Finally, and I don't want to sound pompous - but it sounds like your new to this - are you really sure you want to launch a 2.5Kg payload on your first flight? - my recommendation would be to start with something much lighter.

        We typically launch 2.7 - 3.6 kg payloads using ~800g balloons and 1.5 - 2 k-type tanks of helium. We can get pretty good ascent rates with this setup.

        I found that a good way to think of it is that the balloon size dictates the burst altitude and the amount of helium dictates the lifting force. If you've got a heavy payload (large volume of He) and a small balloon, you'll get a low burst altitude.

        I agree with the above posts, though. Go for the most simple, most lightweight, and cheapest payload possible for your first flight.

                  The simplest way to measure how much gas is in the balloon is to measure the neck lift with weights - tie on the neck lift weight given in the calculator and when you have put in enough gas the weights will lift off the ground (continuously). https://www.flickr.com/photos/16828840@N07/8395853605/47 Don't forget to allow for the weight of the filling tube. The calculator has allowed for the weight of the balloon in the neck lift calculation.

                  There is a calculation that links an estimate of gas needed, neck lift and burst height. given the balloon size, gas, payload weight and ascent rate - its embedded in the online calculator. Its not a straightforward formula - but rather based on an iterative solution process.
